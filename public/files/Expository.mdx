2. During WWII, Vidkun Quisling, Norway’s Minister-President, collaborated with Nazi Germany, facilitating the German invasion of his own country in 1940. He aligned himself with Nazi Germany, declaring himself head of a puppet regime. His name became a synonym for betrayal throughout Europe. After the war, Norway executed him for treason, sealing his legacy as a national disgrace.
3. In 1985, CIA officer Aldrich Ames began spying for the Soviet Union, betraying his country in a devastating way. His actions compromised dozens of agents, leading to the deaths of several operatives and causing significant harm to American intelligence. Astonishingly, Ames’ betrayal went undetected for nearly a decade.
4. After luring Inca Emperor Atahualpa into a false meeting in 1532, Francisco Pizarro demanded a ransom in precious metals. When the gold was delivered, he executed Atahualpa anyway. This act dismantled Inca leadership and allowed Spain to seize control, beginning a brutal colonial legacy that devastated indigenous civilizations.
5. Working as a defense contractor in the 1970s, Boyce accessed U.S. satellite intelligence and passed it to Soviet agents. He escaped prison, funded by bank robberies, and spent two years evading capture. His betrayal revealed how ideological disillusionment could upend national security from inside the wire.
6. Once a key figure in China’s republican movement, Wang sided with the Japanese occupiers and headed their puppet regime in Nanjing. His cooperation fractured China’s wartime resistance. To this day, his name evokes anger and shame among many Chinese historians and citizens.
7. As a senior FBI agent, Hanssen sold classified intelligence to Soviet and Russian handlers for over two decades. His leaks resulted in compromised operations and agent deaths. When arrested in 2001, officials called it one of the most damaging espionage cases in U.S. history.

# Education

## Flaws
1. One of the most significant issues facing the Singaporean education system is the immense stress and pressure placed on students. From a young age, students are pushed to excel academically, often at the expense of their mental health.
2. A study by the OECD found that 86% of Singaporean students reported worrying about poor grades, compared to the OECD average of 66%. Moreover, 76% felt anxious about exams even when well-prepared, significantly higher than the global average of 55%.
3. The National Youth Mental Health Study by the Institute of Mental Health found that about 1 in 3 young people aged between 15 and 35 years in Singapore reported experiencing severe or extremely severe symptoms of depression, anxiety and / or stress. A 2022 national survey found that 25.3% of residents aged 18 to 29 suffered from poor mental health, which was an increase from 21.5% in 2020.
4. In 2016, a 11-year-old boy deliberately committed an act of suicide due to fear of revealing his poor grades to his parents. He had failed multiple subjects during the mid-year examinations. The court heard that the mother would cane the boy's palm lightly, for every mark he fell short of her stipulated standard of 70 per cent.
5. The Singaporean education system is often criticised for its lack of emphasis on creativity and innovation. The system is heavily exam-oriented, which can stifle creativity and discourage innovative thinking. The Global Innovation Index 2016 revealed that creativity was not a widespread trait among Singaporeans — in this study, Singaporeans were found to have underperformed under metrics such as trademarks and creative industry exports.
6. Minister for Education Chan Chun Sing said in parliament that there is correlation between socio-economic background and PSLE results. Advantaged students have a greater availability of educational resources, such as computers, a quiet place to study and an abundance of books at home. Parents with financial means can provide resources such as tuition to enhance their children’s learning, and better provide them with a head-start in life. Without adequate guidance at home, children from less financially stable families are often ill-prepared for learning. These early academic gaps widen when students from higher-income families continue to outperform and move on to schools that have more access to resources.
7. Families in Singapore spent $1.8 billion on private tuition for their children in 2023, as expenditure on education services continues to grow. Despite the Ministry of Education’s (MOE) efforts to reduce academic pressure via recent reforms, a sense of competition is still prevalent among parents and students and has spilt over to the non-academic arena, where the tuition industry has evolved to also cater to students’ non-academic pursuits, such as the emergence of sports academies to prepare students for DSA.

## Benefits
1. Singapore consistently ranks at the top of the OECD's educational assessments, particularly in the PISA tests, where it has outperformed other countries in reading, mathematics, and science literacy. In the 2022 PISA results, Singapore's students achieved the highest average scores, reflecting the effectiveness of its education system and policies.
2. The National University of Singapore (NUS) is currently ranked 8th in the QS World University Rankings 2026 and 17th in the Times Higher Education World University Rankings 2025. It is recognised as the highest-ranked university in Asia.

## Examinations
1. In 2018, MOE toke steps to introduce more flexibility. These include moving away from a rigid T-score in the Primary School Leaving Examination to a wider grade band system, returning to the original aim of recognising non-academic abilities through the Direct School Admission, and adopting aptitude-based entry to higher institutions.
2. In 2023, Mr Chan Chun Sing urged Singaporeans to think beyond themselves and to define success by contribution to society and the well-being of others, rather than individual achievement. He challenged Singaporeans to go beyond excellent Pisa scores and grade performance towards a meritocracy of skills.
3. In 2021, the national examination will do away with the aggregate score - often criticised for being the cause of excessive stress among pupils and parents. With the change, children will no longer be graded relative to one another.
4. Results for national exams, such as the PSLE and A levels, are released without the top scorers being highlighted any more. Naming the PSLE's top scorer ended in 2012. The following year, the Education Ministry (MOE) stopped revealing the highest and lowest scores in the cohort.
5. Schemes to admit students based on their non-academic talents have been introduced. The Direct School Admission (DSA) scheme, which started in 2004, allows secondary schools to admit students based on not just grades but their sporting and artistic merits. At NUS, for instance, freshmen are able to omit grades of some modules in their first semester. All five polytechnics and three of the local universities - NTU, SMU and NUS - will be expanding their aptitude-based admissions, allowing students who do not quite make the grade academically but show passion and interest to get into the course of their choice.
6. MOE announced in September 2018 that there would be no more mid-year exams for Primary 3 to 5 pupils as well as Secondary 1 and 3 students by 2021, to move away from the narrow focus of grades and help school children discover their joy for learning.

## Learning
1. Singapore mandated the study of a second language in all  schools, leading the introduction of the teaching of three mother tongues - Chinese, Malay and Tamil - in English-medium schools. Such a bilingual policy has proven to be successful, and has paved the way for English to become the common working language, breaking down language barriers and allowing individual Singaporeans to be able to communicate and negotiate with international businessmen.
2. The Applied Learning Programme (ALP) will play a key role in encouraging students to explore ideas, try out new skills such as basic coding, and be creative, said Education Minister Ng Chee Meng. The programme emphasise the application of thinking skills and connecting knowledge across subject disciplines, in areas including design, robotics and journalism.
3. The gifted programme, a curriculum catering to intellectually gifted students, will be overhauled and replaced with a new approach for high-ability learners in all primary schools. “Every primary school will have its own programmes to stretch these students in their areas of strength and interest.” Such students will no longer have to transfer to a school offering the GEP, but can continue in their schools and "remain with friends and teachers whom they have bonded with", Mr Wong said.
4. The Code for Fun programme, offered jointly by IMDA and Ministry of Education (MOE), aims to expose primary and secondary school students to computational thinking through coding, inventive thinking through digital making and emerging technologies such as Artificial Intelligence (AI). The programme is available to all MOE government and government-aided primary and secondary schools.
5. MOE is also placing a stronger emphasis on outdoor education. Under a physical education syllabus introduced in 2014, 10 to 20 per cent of curriculum time in primary and secondary schools is set aside for outdoor education. As part of a new National Outdoor Adventure Education Masterplan, all students from 2020 will also take part in three cohort camps during their school years, including a five-day Outward Bound Singapore camp for Secondary Three students. At Primary Four or Five, pupils take part in a three-day cohort camp and learn to prepare simple meals, adapt to the outdoors, and build resilience and camaraderie.
6. MOE is transforming education through technology with the EdTech Masterplan 2030, focusing on digital literacy, AI literacy, and enhancing 21st-century competencies. This includes tools like the Language Feedback Assistant and Short Answer Feedback Assistant for teachers, and resources on the Singapore Student Learning Space. Additionally, a $64 million grant will support schools in transforming learning spaces to meet evolving educational needs.
7. As part of the EdTech Masterplan 2030, MOE in June 2023 launched the Adaptive Learning System (ALS) for Mathematics for three topics covered at the Primary 5 level. The artificial intelligence-enabled ALS makes customised learning recommendations for each pupil, based on how the pupil responds to the questions and activities.

## Alternative
1. Martin Luther King Jr. argues that education has both a utilitarian and a moral function, asserting that reasoning ability is not enough. He insists that character and moral development are necessary to give the critical intellect for humane purposes.
2. The University of York found that parents' socioeconomic status and children's inherited DNA differences are powerful predictors of educational achievement. Children with a high genetic propensity for education who were also from wealthy and well-educated family backgrounds had the greatest advantage with 77% going to University.
3. The Coleman Report found that academic achievement was influenced more by the social composition of the school and family background than by the school's resources.
4. Finnish teachers are highly trained and respected professionals, with most holding a master's degree in education. Finland's education system emphasised equity over excellence, providing equal opportunities to all students regardless of their background or abilities. There is no standardised testing in Finland. All children are graded on an individualised basis and grading system set by their teacher.
5. As one of a handful of countries that offers free tuition to college students, Denmark grants students enormous freedom from debt and the pressure to declare a major right away. The Folkeskole Act requires schools to differentiate education based on students' needs, ensuring that all children receive appropriate support.
6. A report last year by the World Economic Forum titled New Vision of Education said that to thrive in today's innovation-driven economy, employees need a different mix of skills than in the past. "In addition to foundational skills like literacy and numeracy, they need competencies like collaboration, creativity and problem-solving, and character qualities like persistence, curiosity and initiative," the report said.
7. In July 2021, the China government launched a sweeping clampdown on its private tuition sector, banning them from providing for-profit classes to ease the burden on families, including overworked students and parents struggling to pay tuition. However, tuition actually rose for many households. Parents eager to give their kids a head start in education say they have turned to expensive, underground tuition services that have mushroomed across the country.  The system of entrance exams, in which high schools and colleges admit students solely based on scores from once-a-year tests, still prevails. China’s college entrance exam is notoriously competitive, with more than 10 million students taking exams each year.
8. Albert Einstein's conflicted with the rigid, authoritarian nature of traditional schools, which he found stifling and unsuited to his curiosity-driven, self-directed learning style. He chafed under rote memorisation, skipped classes he found boring, and clashed with teachers who prioritised obedience over understanding.
9. Thomas Edison's struggled in traditional school settings due to difficulties such as dyslexia and hearing loss. He found traditional schools inattentive and difficult, with a teacher once calling him "slow" and "addled". His mother, Nancy Edison, removed him from school and provided a personalised homeschooling environment that nurtured his curiosity and creativity. He taught himself by constantly reading and conducting chemical experiments.
10. Richard Branson's struggled with dyslexia and ADHD, which led to poor academic performance and difficulty in traditional school settings, ultimately leading him to leave school early.

## Volunteerism

1. Values in Action (VIA) in Singapore requires secondary school students to complete at least six hours of community service annually. The National Volunteer and Philanthropy Centre noted an increase in the volunteerism rate among youth from 23% in 2021 to 31% in 2023.
2. The President’s Challenge encourages Singaporeans to volunteer or fundraise for social causes, such as special needs education and healthcare. In 2022, over 50,000 volunteers contributed 300,000 hours to support underserved communities.
3. AmeriCorps in the United States engages young adults in year-long projects across education, health, and disaster response. In 2021, members provided over 29 million service hours, reaching more than 1 million Americans.
4. Roots of Empathy in Canada pairs students with infants and caregivers to teach empathy and social responsibility. Studies report a 40% reduction in bullying in participating classrooms.
5. South Africa’s Youth Volunteer Programme mobilises students for HIV awareness, environmental conservation, and health initiatives. In 2024, volunteers participated in campaigns including the Red Run marathon to raise HIV/AIDS awareness.
6. Japanese high school Volunteer Club Programs encourage students to assist in disaster relief, environmental projects, and eldercare. About 60–70% of students participate, building leadership, collaboration, and empathy skills.
